## What They Do

Plant with Willow (trading as Willow) is a Perth-based consumer hardware and software startup that makes smart plant care technology. Their product helps everyday Australians — self-described "serial plant killers" — keep their houseplants alive by removing the guesswork from plant care.

**Core product capabilities:**

- **Willow Sensor** — a 4-in-1 IoT device inserted into plant soil that continuously monitors soil moisture, ambient light, temperature, and humidity in real time
- **Willow Hub** — a WiFi-connected hub that bridges up to 40 sensors to the app; connects via Bluetooth to the sensor and home 2.4GHz WiFi
- **Willow App (iOS & Android)** — translates sensor data into simple, actionable alerts and care recommendations tailored to each plant species; includes a plant score, event logging, photo diary, and plant library
- **Plant identification** — AI-powered photo ID to identify unknown plants from a snapshot
- **Willow Grow (premium tier)** — paid subscription unlocking advanced sensor data, access to horticulturist advice, disease identification, and a plant matcher feature
- **Willow Marketplace** — in-app marketplace for plant care products
- **Location-based care** — considers seasonality, local weather, and plant dormancy for more accurate advice

The product ships from Perth and Melbourne warehouses, and is available online via their own website and through Bunnings stores nationally. It has shipped to over 25 countries. Willow won a Good Design Award for the Sensor product.

## Who Their Customers Are

**Primary customers:** Australian consumers — specifically those who love plants but struggle with keeping them alive; self-identified as the 72 million indoor plants killed by Aussies each year.

**Key customer profiles:**
- Casual houseplant owners who are plant-curious but lack confidence
- Plant enthusiasts wanting data-driven care across larger collections (up to 40 sensors per hub)
- Gift buyers — the product packages well as a premium gifting item
- Bunnings shoppers browsing the garden and nursery category

**Geographic reach:**
- Primary market: Australia (Perth and Melbourne distribution)
- International: shipped to 25+ countries; a UK-based operation also appears to be active under the same brand
- Retail presence: Bunnings stores nationally (in-store rollout from 2025 after initial online availability from July 2024)

## Founder Background

**Jared Carlin — Co-Founder**

Jared brings over 12 years of experience in product innovation — identifying, incubating, and commercialising new products. A Western Australian native and Curtin University graduate, he has a deep background in translating ideas into physical products. At Willow, Jared leads the hardware and product development side of the business. His personal frustration with killing houseplants was the original spark for the product.

**James Shamim — Co-Founder**

James (Jimmy) is a commercially minded generalist with a background spanning business development, finance, commercial, and operations across public, private, government, and startup environments. He entered the tech industry in 2016 and was part of a Deloitte Fast 50 award-winning tech company before co-founding Willow. He leads the commercial and business side of the startup.

**Origin Story**

Willow was born during the 2020 lockdown period, when Jared and James found themselves spending more time at home — and accumulating more plants than they could keep alive. After speaking with friends, family, and Spacecubed community members and finding the problem was universal, they set out to build a hardware solution from scratch. They joined BetterLabs' venture studio, giving them access to funding, frameworks, and a network of fellow founders to validate and accelerate the business. The Kickstarter in November 2021 doubled its goal with global backers, validating the product commercially before going to retail.

## Competitors & Market Position

Willow operates in the global smart plant care and houseplant tech market — a growing category sitting at the intersection of IoT, consumer hardware, and the houseplant boom.

| Company | What They Do | Relationship |
|---|---|---|
| **Planta** | Popular plant care app with premium subscription; no physical sensor | App-only competitor — strong international user base |
| **Greg** | Plant care app with community features and watering schedules | App-only competitor — known for accurate plant ID |
| **Parrot (Flower Power)** | Early smart plant sensor from French tech company | Hardware predecessor — established but dated product |
| **Xiaomi / HHCC Plant Monitor** | Low-cost smart plant sensor from China | Budget hardware competitor; lacks species-specific insights |
| **Sustee** | Simple soil moisture indicator (no app, no data) | Lower-tech alternative targeting casual buyers |
| **Click & Grow / AeroGarden** | Smart indoor growing systems | Adjacent category — hydroponic gardens rather than care for existing plants |

**Willow's market position:** Willow sits at the premium end of the smart plant sensor category, differentiating on species-specific insights rather than generic readings. Where a cheap moisture sensor just gives a percentage reading, Willow interprets what that number means for that specific plant, factoring in seasonality and care history. The Bunnings distribution deal is a significant competitive moat — it places Willow in front of millions of Australians at point-of-plant-purchase, a natural and powerful moment of relevance.

## Pain Points

- **Hardware complexity** — producing and scaling a physical IoT device is significantly more capital-intensive and operationally complex than a pure software business
- **App quality gap** — some user reviews flag pairing difficulties, missing plants from the library, and iPad incompatibility, suggesting product polish still needs work
- **Consumer hardware margin pressure** — physical products carry COGS, freight, warehousing, and returns costs that compress margins compared to SaaS
- **Retail dependency** — landing Bunnings is a win, but it also creates dependency on a single major retail partner
- **International expansion** — shipping hardware globally is logistically challenging; building brand awareness in new markets without local retail presence is slow and expensive
- **Subscription conversion** — converting free app users to the paid Willow Grow tier is a key revenue lever that requires ongoing product investment
- **Plant library gaps** — the care database needs continuous expansion to cover the full range of plants customers own

## Red Flags & Risks

| Risk | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Hardware scaling risk** | Manufacturing and inventory management at scale is difficult; supply chain disruptions can hit hard |
| **Retail concentration** | Heavy reliance on Bunnings as a retail partner — any change to that relationship would significantly impact distribution |
| **Competitive intensity** | The app-only competitors (Planta, Greg) have larger user bases and lower cost structures |
| **Low-cost hardware substitutes** | Cheap sensors from Xiaomi and other Asian manufacturers undercut on price |
| **App experience issues** | Negative reviews around pairing, missing plants, and iPad support could hurt conversion and retention |
| **Small team** | A lean founding team managing hardware, software, marketing, and retail simultaneously is a high-risk operational model |
| **BetterLabs dependency** | As a venture studio company, strategic and funding decisions are partly shaped by BetterLabs' priorities |
| **Consumer market volatility** | The houseplant boom of 2020–2021 may normalise; discretionary spending on plant tech is sensitive to economic conditions |

## SWOT Analysis

### Strengths

- Solves a real, relatable, and widely felt problem — 72 million plants killed annually in Australia alone
- Physical hardware + app ecosystem creates a defensible, sticky product (not easily replicated by app-only competitors)
- Bunnings distribution — national retail presence in Australia's most visited hardware and garden retailer
- Good Design Award winner — third-party validation of product quality and aesthetics
- BetterLabs / RAC WA backing — institutional support, funding, and a network of fellow founders
- Strong early customer reviews and word-of-mouth traction
- International shipping to 25+ countries demonstrates global product-market fit signals
- Species-specific, data-contextualised insights are a genuine differentiator from low-cost sensor alternatives

### Weaknesses

- Hardware business model is capital-intensive with thinner margins than pure software
- App product still has quality gaps that affect user experience and reviews
- Small team stretched across hardware, software, marketing, and retail operations
- Plant library still growing — gaps reduce value for owners of less common plants
- Limited brand awareness outside Australia

### Opportunities

- Global smart plant sensor market projected to reach USD 2.5 billion by 2030 (CAGR ~17.5%)
- Expansion into UK and European markets — brand already has early traction
- Willow Grow subscription is an underdeveloped revenue stream with strong upside
- Partnerships with nurseries, garden centres, and online plant retailers beyond Bunnings
- Corporate and gifting channels — the product packages well and solves a universal office plant problem
- Smart home integration (HomeKit, Home Assistant) could unlock a broader IoT-savvy audience
- Growing demand for wellness and biophilic design in homes and workplaces

### Threats

- App-only competitors with larger user bases and no hardware COGS could close the feature gap
- Low-cost hardware from Chinese manufacturers commoditising the sensor category
- Consumer spending tightening — smart plant sensors are a discretionary purchase
- Post-pandemic houseplant boom normalising, reducing category growth tailwinds
- Retail landscape risk — any change to Bunnings partnership terms significantly impacts the business
- New entrants from established smart home brands (e.g. Xiaomi ecosystem, Amazon) entering plant care
